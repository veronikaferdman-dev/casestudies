Case Study — Teaching an AI Model to "Show Care"

An AI model can learn grammar. Teaching it to recognize whether content treats people with care is a different problem entirely.

How a subjective content-design heuristic became a standard precise enough to train and grade an AI model against.

The Problem

Meta's internal Design Agent — an AI tool that suggests rewrites to product strings — could tell whether a rewrite was grammatically correct and on-brand. What it couldn't reliably tell was whether a rewrite actually showed care for the person reading it: whether it anticipated confusion, acknowledged what was at stake for the user, or avoided jargon a stressed person wouldn't parse in the moment.

"Shows care" is a standard content designers apply by instinct, built from years of reading interfaces the way a real, distracted, possibly anxious user would. It had never been written down precisely enough for a model to be trained or graded against — two rewrites could both be technically correct, and only a content designer's eye could tell you which one actually respected the reader.

The Approach

01

Define the standard in gradable terms

Translated "shows care" from an instinct into concrete criteria — naming what separates a rewrite that respects the reader's context from one that's merely correct.

02

Build a labeled example set

Created paired examples of content that did and didn't meet the standard, giving the model concrete cases to reason from instead of an abstract principle to guess at.

03

Evaluate the model's actual suggestions

Reviewed AI-suggested string rewrites against the standard, identifying not just where the model's reasoning broke down, but why.

04

Refine the logic and re-test

Fed those failure patterns back into the model's reasoning and its application of CD standards, then re-evaluated performance against Meta's Beta Entry bar.

What I Did

  • StandardOperationalized "shows care," a qualitative content-craft heuristic, into criteria precise enough for a model to be trained and evaluated against.
  • EvaluationPersonally reviewed AI-suggested rewrites, grading the model's outputs and identifying systematic gaps in its reasoning.
  • ScaleExtended this work as one of three Content Design leads in Meta's AI String Manager Pilot Program, reviewing roughly 1,500 strings to improve AI tooling used across the broader CD organization.
  • IterationRefined the model's reasoning and standards application based on review findings, then re-tested against the 2025 H2 modeling goal.

What This Demonstrates

This is the place where content design and AI evaluation stop being two different skill sets and become the same discipline. Defining "shows care" required the same instinct a content designer brings to a single screen: reading content the way a real, possibly stressed, possibly confused person would. Turning that instinct into labeled examples, evaluation criteria, and a precision/recall target required treating content judgment as a system precise enough to specify, test, and improve — the same rigor as any other quality bar.

Most people are strong in one half of this: the craft judgment, or the measurement rigor. Doing both — defining what "good" means, then proving a model actually learned it — is what lets content design judgment scale past a single string and into the systems an AI product runs on.
